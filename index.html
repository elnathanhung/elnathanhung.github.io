Elnathan is a designer with a background in interactive media. His expertise lies in designing environments and experiences within various spectrums of interaction; physical products and experiences, immersive spaces, virtual environments, augmented and mixed realities, and conventional and speculative UI/UX design. His practice includes making and programming robots, game environments, websites, application prototypes, and exhibition designs.

“Retro Future” is an exploration game run on Unreal Engine 4. Imagine reliving your day over and over again. In a post apocalyptic future, robots dominate over society. Humans are left to die while cyborgs and robots triumph. Due to the war, the world becomes self-destructive and civilisation is soon brought to extinction.

Our protagonist needs to find a way to prevent the invention of AI from ever happening. With time-travel technology, he need to find the creator and convince him/her to stop the invention.

The Empathy Bot is an AI assistant that is able to detect human emotions based on facial expression. It is aware of presences and invites users to interact with it. A happy face would trigger cause the robot to dance and whistle mimicking a positive feeling. However, an upset expression causes the robot to bow its head, expressing sadness.

3D modeled parts were strategically designed and printed to stabilize and fit into the construction of the robot that functions as a simple pan and tilt mechanism. The robot is programmed to surveillance its surroundings through a webcam while reading information from the user's facial expression. The information is sent to a computer via OSC communication while the different detected emotional states are trained through software. An emotional state triggers a reaction from the robot, thus resiprocating an emotion to the user.

“Nostalgia Reworked” is a speculative design for an exhibition installation that mixes elements of nostalgia with expressions of the contemporary. The project was designed to be situated within the Nanyang Technological University, School of Art, Design and Media Gallery (ADM Gallery), for an exhibition with the theme of “Singapore Dubbing”. This theme is inspired by the notion of Dub music; the blending of two ‘genres’ of spaces.

Within the exhibition model, we see the dubbing of two points of view within the space; on one side we see visual elements from olden-day Singapore (e.g. shophouse windows), while on the other, we see hints of modern design (e.g. the Bauhaus Globe chair). As a result, the viewers walk into a space not exactly knowing which point in time they have entered.

The exhibition installation includes a soundscape demonstrating the different reminiscences triggered by the nostalgic objects found. The video demonstration is a prototype solution for a speculative interactive component designed for the soundscape. Visitors take on the role of 'the conductor' as they have control over the atmospheric landscape. I have selected a total of three nostalgic elements.

The first is a radio which corresponds to the audio broadcast of an old Singaporean tune ("Bengawang Solo" by The Sundowners). Next, is one retro television, which triggers the sound of a Singaporean TV broadcast 50 years ago. Lastly, we have a traditional round marble table, which activates the sound from roadsides of old coffee shops (or 'kopitiams') in the early morning. Through the gesture of pointing from one object to the next, visitors within the space can select and tune in to their desired soundscape.

This interaction prototype was programmed with Mediapipe Python, while the data training was done on Wekinator and TouchDesigner.

“Monkey See, Monkey Do” refers to the act of imitation with the limited knowledge of consequences or reason. Playing on the idea that a small ripple can create a tsunami, Whackinator serves as a commentary on the societal need for inclusion and the hive mind mentality. The hands are sets of servo motors programmed with Raspberry Pi computers while the hand sensing is through Python Mediapipe which communicates to the servos via OSC message.

"Paya Lebar Airbase: the place to be… but we can’t" is an interactive website entailing history and personal encounters within the location, Paya Lebar Airbase (PLAB) in Singapore. As usual protocols for high-security military airbases would suggest, PLAB is blocked from the public eye apart from open house event coverages and images posted on the internet (which are mostly given security clearance). Through this interactive narrative, I experiment with how one can experience a place that one cannot enter or see.

This project pushes the boundaries of what we can find or document about disclosed locations. The entire website is developed and coded from scratch. Chapter 1 documents some of the histories of PLAB, chapter 2 is an imagining of the experience of the location as I narrate my personal experiences within the base, and lastly, chapter 3 is an interactive activity where I invite you to plan the future of PLAB.

The Empathy Bot is conceptually an AI assistant that is able to empathize with human emotions based on facial expression. It is aware of presences and invites users to interact with it. A happy face would trigger cause the robot to dance and whistle mimicking a positive feeling. However, an upset expression causes the robot to bow its head, expressing sadness.

In order to build a simple pan and tilt mechanism, 3D modeled parts were strategically designed and printed to stabilize and fit into the construction of the robot. The robot surveillances through a webcam while reading information from the user's facial expression, and sends information via OSC communication. The different emotional states are thus read and the robot reacts accordingly.

“Nostalgia Reworked” is a speculative design for an exhibition installation that mixes elements of nostalgia with expressions of the contemporary. The project was designed to be situated within the Nanyang Technological University, School of Art, Design and Media Gallery, for an exhibition with the theme of “Singapore Dubbing”. This theme is inspired by the notion of Dub music; the blending of two ‘genres’ of spaces. Within the exhibition model, we see the dubbing of two points of view within the space; on one side we see visual elements from olden-day Singapore (e.g. shophouse windows), while on the other, we see hints of modern design (e.g. the Bauhaus Globe chair). As a result, the viewers walk into a space not exactly knowing which point in time they have entered.

“Monkey See, Monkey Do” refers to the act of imitation with the limited knowledge of consequences or reason. Playing on the idea that a small ripple can create a tsunami, Whackinator serves as a commentary on the societal need for inclusion and the hive mind mentality. The hands are sets of servo motors programmed with Raspberry Pi computers while the hand sensing is through Python Mediapipe which communicates to the servos via OSC message.

"Paya Lebar Airbase: the place to be… but we can’t" is an interactive website entailing history and personal encounters within the location, Paya Lebar Airbase (PLAB) in Singapore. As usual protocols for high-security military airbases would suggest, PLAB is blocked from the public eye apart from open house event coverages and images posted on the internet (which are mostly given security clearance). Through this interactive narrative, I experiment with how one can experience a place where one cannot enter or see.

This project pushes the boundaries of what we can find or document about a confidential location. The entire website is developed and coded from scratch. Chapter 1 documents some of the histories of PLAB, chapter 2 is an imagining of the experience of the location as I narrate my personal experiences within the base, and lastly, chapter 3 is an interactive activity where I invite you to plan the future of PLAB.
